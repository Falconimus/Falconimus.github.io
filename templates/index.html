What is Eartraining?

This website was made so that people could have an automated way of practicing their hearing,
this is especially useful to musicians who because of their studies need to be able to distinctively
identify different Notes and Intervals.

What exercises are there?

In Eartraining we have two different modes (Intervals and Absolute) with 3 different
difficulties (Easy, Medium, Hard).

What does the "Absolute" exercise consist of?

In the "Absolute" exercise the user must simply recognize (by hearing) the note that is being played.
As stated earlier there are three difficulties; Easy, Medium and Hard. The difference between the difficulties
lies in the number of notes that can be played and must therefore be recognized.
Of course in order to understand and practice here one must know the notes and their names,
for anyone who can´t read notes here is a guide to english notes,
and here is a guide to spanish notes. If you go to the "Sounds" section of this website you can also listen to any notes in particular.

How does the "Intervals" exercise work?

In the "Intervals" exercise 2 notes are selected and played one after another, and then together.
The user´s task is going to be to recognize the Interval ("distance") between the 2 notes and
the direction (Ascending, Descending, Unison).
In order to complete this exercise the user must know how Intervals are named, here are some examples:
Do/C to MI/E would be a Major third (M3/3M), since there are 2 full notes of difference.
To fully understand this, one must know that a Major second (M2/2M), would mean one full note
of difference (say DO/C and RE/D) and the minor second (m2/2m) would be half a note difference (say DO/C and DO#/#).
If you don´t know all Interval names, or you want to listen to any Interval in particular you can go to the "Sounds" section
of this website.
